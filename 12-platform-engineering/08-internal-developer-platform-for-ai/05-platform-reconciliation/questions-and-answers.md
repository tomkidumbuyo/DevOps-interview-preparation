## Junior — normal conceptual and code questions

### PLATFORM-RECONCILIATION-JN-01

- [ ] **Question:** What is Desired state, and why does it matter in Platform reconciliation?
> **Covered in:** [Platform reconciliation — Desired state](README.md#desired-state)

**Answer:** **Definition.** The term Desired state refers to a platform-product mechanism that turns a user intent into a governed, supported and observable capability within Platform reconciliation. **Mechanism.** A write normally passes validation and authorization, enters a buffer or transaction, reaches a durable medium, and may then replicate or become visible to readers. Caches and indexes accelerate access but introduce freshness and eviction behavior; snapshots preserve a point-in-time representation but application consistency depends on write ordering. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence combines capacity, latency, error, replication and integrity signals with a tested read or restore. Typical failures include exhausted bytes or inodes, throttling, stale replicas, corrupt metadata, topology mismatch, lost encryption keys and backups that exist but cannot reconstruct the application within RPO/RTO. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Desired state matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Platform reconciliation journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### PLATFORM-RECONCILIATION-JN-07

- [ ] **Question:** What is Partial failure, and why does it matter in Platform reconciliation?
> **Covered in:** [Platform reconciliation — Partial failure](README.md#partial-failure)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Means one component or message can fail while others continue, so a timeout cannot prove whether a remote side effect occurred. **Mechanism.** A caller assigns a deadline and attempt budget, classifies an error, applies bounded backoff with jitter only when retry is safe, and propagates capacity limits upstream. Circuit breakers, bulkheads and load shedding prevent one dependency from consuming every worker; recovery probes and gradual traffic return avoid a second overload. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows bounded queues, stable retry ratios and recovery within an objective. Unbounded retries, identical timeouts at every layer, non-idempotent replays, shared resource pools and failover to an unqualified target commonly turn a small fault into an outage. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Partial failure matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Platform reconciliation journey rather than an administrative green status alone.
