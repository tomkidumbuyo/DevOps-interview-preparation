- [ ] **Question:** What is Availability Zone failure, and why does it matter in Reliability scenarios?
> **Covered in:** [Reliability scenarios — Availability Zone failure](README.md#availability-zone-failure)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Requires a layer-by-layer, evidence-first path from user impact and recent change through identity, configuration, runtime, dependency and resource saturation, followed by reversible mitigation and verified repair. **Mechanism.** A caller assigns a deadline and attempt budget, classifies an error, applies bounded backoff with jitter only when retry is safe, and propagates capacity limits upstream. Circuit breakers, bulkheads and load shedding prevent one dependency from consuming every worker; recovery probes and gradual traffic return avoid a second overload. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows bounded queues, stable retry ratios and recovery within an objective. Unbounded retries, identical timeouts at every layer, non-idempotent replays, shared resource pools and failover to an unqualified target commonly turn a small fault into an outage. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Availability Zone failure matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Reliability scenarios journey rather than an administrative green status alone.
