## Junior — normal conceptual and code questions

### KUBERNETES-OBSERVABILITY-JN-01

- [ ] **Question:** What is kube-state-metrics, and why does it matter in Kubernetes observability?
> **Covered in:** [Kubernetes observability — kube-state-metrics](README.md#kube-state-metrics)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Exposes object desired/status metrics rather than container usage. **Mechanism.** Instrumentation observes a defined event or state, attaches bounded context, transports the signal, stores it under retention rules and makes it queryable. A useful signal has documented semantics and can distinguish at least two competing explanations; correlation identifiers belong in logs or traces rather than unbounded metric labels. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence includes coverage of the user journey, known collection delay and actionable SLO or saturation views. Missing telemetry, sampling bias, cardinality explosions, sensitive payload capture and alert thresholds disconnected from user impact are failures of the observability system itself. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which kube-state-metrics matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Kubernetes observability journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### KUBERNETES-OBSERVABILITY-JN-03

- [ ] **Question:** What is kubelet/cAdvisor, and why does it matter in Kubernetes observability?
> **Covered in:** [Kubernetes observability — kubelet/cAdvisor](README.md#kubeletcadvisor)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Node/container CPU/memory/filesystem/network metrics under version/runtime coverage. **Mechanism.** The mechanism receives configuration or runtime input, validates preconditions, changes or derives state, and exposes status to the next component. Synchronous parts return a direct outcome; asynchronous parts need durable status, reconciliation and idempotency because the original caller may disappear. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows the expected state transition and its owner, timestamp and reason. Invalid input, stale state, conflicting writers, hidden limits and dependencies that partially complete are the most useful failure categories to distinguish. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which kubelet/cAdvisor matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Kubernetes observability journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### KUBERNETES-OBSERVABILITY-JN-06

- [ ] **Question:** What is Audit log, and why does it matter in Kubernetes observability?
> **Covered in:** [Kubernetes observability — Audit log](README.md#audit-log)

**Answer:** **Definition.** API request identity/verb/resource/stage evidence with privacy/volume policy. **Mechanism.** At runtime a caller presents or derives an identity, the enforcement point gathers identity, resource and request context, and applicable rules produce allow or deny. The effective decision is the intersection of all guardrails; encryption protects bytes but does not replace authorization, and audit records explain which decision was made. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence includes a short-lived attributable identity, narrowly scoped access and an audit event for the intended resource. Failures commonly come from expired credentials, mismatched trust, an overriding deny, wrong resource scope or key/certificate lifecycle problems; widening access may hide the cause while creating a breach path. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Audit log matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Kubernetes observability journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### KUBERNETES-OBSERVABILITY-JN-08

- [ ] **Code:** **Question:** What does `kubectl logs -n monitoring deploy/prometheus --since=30m` help you verify for Kubernetes observability?
> **Covered in:** [Kubernetes observability — Worked command and configuration example](README.md#worked-command-and-configuration-example)

**Answer:** **Purpose.** `kubectl logs -n monitoring deploy/prometheus --since=30m` reads Kubernetes API desired/status state or recent reconciliation evidence without treating a single `Ready` value as proof of the user journey. **Mechanism.** The relevant Kubernetes observability concept is Network telemetry: DNS/CNI/conntrack/eBPF/flow/LB signals locate service paths. The command reads one representation of desired or observed state; it does not by itself prove that every controller, dependency or user path is healthy. **Evidence.** Confirm identity, account/project/cluster, location and exact resource first, then preserve IDs, status/reason fields, timestamps and exit status. Compare them with source-controlled intent and a known healthy baseline, and correlate the nearest event, metric, log, trace or audit record. **Failure and trade-off.** Empty output, permission denial, stale state and an unavailable dependency require different next checks; immediately mutating the target destroys evidence and may widen impact. **Production example.** During a Kubernetes observability rollout, an operator uses this check to decide whether the fault is in accepted configuration, reconciliation or the live data path, and verifies the final repair from the original client.

### KUBERNETES-OBSERVABILITY-MN-01

- [ ] **Question:** Compare kube-state-metrics with Metrics Server. When would each change your design?
> **Covered in:** [Kubernetes observability — Metrics Server](README.md#metrics-server)

**Answer:** **Definitions.** kube-state-metrics: Exposes object desired/status metrics rather than container usage. Metrics Server: Lightweight resource metrics for top/HPA, not a long-term monitoring backend. **Mechanism.** In Kubernetes observability, compare where each acts in the request or resource lifecycle, which state it owns, whether its result is synchronous or eventually reconciled, and how callers observe completion. They may be complementary layers rather than substitutes. **Decision criteria.** Select using workload shape and protocol, identity and tenant isolation, failure-domain independence, consistency and recovery contract, latency/throughput limits, team ownership and total cost. **Failure evidence.** State which metric, event, status field or controlled test would distinguish failure of one from the other, and how rollback differs. **Production example.** For a production Kubernetes observability design, use the simpler option until measured SLO, isolation, scale or recovery evidence crosses an explicit threshold; document that threshold so the decision can be revisited instead of becoming permanent folklore.

Correlate identity, desired revision, events, SLI, dependency state and capacity; never make a destructive move from a single green or red field. **Mechanism and risk.** Exposes object desired/status metrics rather than container usage.
