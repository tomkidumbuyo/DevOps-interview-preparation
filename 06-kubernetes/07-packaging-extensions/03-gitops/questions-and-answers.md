## Junior — normal conceptual and code questions

### ARGO-CD-FLUX-AND-GITOPS-JN-01

- [ ] **Question:** What is Desired source, and why does it matter in Argo CD, Flux and GitOps?
> **Covered in:** [Argo CD, Flux and GitOps — Desired source](README.md#desired-source)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Git/OCI revision plus render inputs is the audit/reconciliation truth. **Mechanism.** The mechanism receives configuration or runtime input, validates preconditions, changes or derives state, and exposes status to the next component. Synchronous parts return a direct outcome; asynchronous parts need durable status, reconciliation and idempotency because the original caller may disappear. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows the expected state transition and its owner, timestamp and reason. Invalid input, stale state, conflicting writers, hidden limits and dependencies that partially complete are the most useful failure categories to distinguish. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Desired source matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Argo CD, Flux and GitOps journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### ARGO-CD-FLUX-AND-GITOPS-JN-03

- [ ] **Question:** What is Health, and why does it matter in Argo CD, Flux and GitOps?
> **Covered in:** [Argo CD, Flux and GitOps — Health](README.md#health)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Resource-specific status estimates rollout but needs user/SLO verification. **Mechanism.** A caller assigns a deadline and attempt budget, classifies an error, applies bounded backoff with jitter only when retry is safe, and propagates capacity limits upstream. Circuit breakers, bulkheads and load shedding prevent one dependency from consuming every worker; recovery probes and gradual traffic return avoid a second overload. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows bounded queues, stable retry ratios and recovery within an objective. Unbounded retries, identical timeouts at every layer, non-idempotent replays, shared resource pools and failover to an unqualified target commonly turn a small fault into an outage. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Health matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Argo CD, Flux and GitOps journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### ARGO-CD-FLUX-AND-GITOPS-JN-08

- [ ] **Code:** **Question:** What does `argocd app wait APP --health --sync` help you verify for Argo CD, Flux and GitOps?
> **Covered in:** [Argo CD, Flux and GitOps — Health](README.md#health)

**Answer:** **Purpose.** `argocd app wait APP --health --sync` captures a read-oriented state snapshot that must be interpreted against a healthy baseline, the exact target and the next adjacent dependency. **Mechanism.** The relevant Argo CD, Flux and GitOps concept is Secret workflow: Encrypted/external secret references prevent plaintext Git while preserving rotation. The command reads one representation of desired or observed state; it does not by itself prove that every controller, dependency or user path is healthy. **Evidence.** Confirm identity, account/project/cluster, location and exact resource first, then preserve IDs, status/reason fields, timestamps and exit status. Compare them with source-controlled intent and a known healthy baseline, and correlate the nearest event, metric, log, trace or audit record. **Failure and trade-off.** Empty output, permission denial, stale state and an unavailable dependency require different next checks; immediately mutating the target destroys evidence and may widen impact. **Production example.** During a Argo CD, Flux and GitOps rollout, an operator uses this check to decide whether the fault is in accepted configuration, reconciliation or the live data path, and verifies the final repair from the original client.
