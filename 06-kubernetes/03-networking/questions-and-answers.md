## Junior — normal conceptual and code questions

### BRANCH-NETWORKING-JN-01

- [ ] **Question:** What is ClusterIP, and why does it matter in Networking?
> **Covered in:** [Networking — ClusterIP](README.md#clusterip)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Virtual internal IP implemented by the cluster service data plane. **Mechanism.** The mechanism receives configuration or runtime input, validates preconditions, changes or derives state, and exposes status to the next component. Synchronous parts return a direct outcome; asynchronous parts need durable status, reconciliation and idempotency because the original caller may disappear. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows the expected state transition and its owner, timestamp and reason. Invalid input, stale state, conflicting writers, hidden limits and dependencies that partially complete are the most useful failure categories to distinguish. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which ClusterIP matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Networking journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### BRANCH-NETWORKING-JN-02

- [ ] **Question:** What is NodePort, and why does it matter in Networking?
> **Covered in:** [Networking — NodePort](README.md#nodeport)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Reserves a port on nodes and routes to Service endpoints. **Mechanism.** A real request crosses several independent states: name resolution returns an address, the source selects a route and source address, link or overlay forwarding reaches the next hop, stateful or stateless policy evaluates the flow, transport establishes communication, and TLS/application protocols negotiate their own contract. The return path must also work. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence progresses layer by layer: correct name/address, expected route, permitted flow, listening endpoint, successful handshake and valid application response. Timeouts, refusals, resets and protocol errors mean different layers; packet loss, MTU, asymmetric paths, connection-state exhaustion and proxy timeout mismatch are common production failures. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which NodePort matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Networking journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

> **Covered in:** [Networking — ClusterIP](README.md#clusterip)

**Answer:** **Definitions.** ClusterIP: Virtual internal IP implemented by the cluster service data plane. NodePort: Reserves a port on nodes and routes to Service endpoints. **Mechanism.** In Networking, compare where each acts in the request or resource lifecycle, which state it owns, whether its result is synchronous or eventually reconciled, and how callers observe completion. They may be complementary layers rather than substitutes. **Decision criteria.** Select using workload shape and protocol, identity and tenant isolation, failure-domain independence, consistency and recovery contract, latency/throughput limits, team ownership and total cost. **Failure evidence.** State which metric, event, status field or controlled test would distinguish failure of one from the other, and how rollback differs. **Production example.** For a production Networking design, use the simpler option until measured SLO, isolation, scale or recovery evidence crosses an explicit threshold; document that threshold so the decision can be revisited instead of becoming permanent folklore.
