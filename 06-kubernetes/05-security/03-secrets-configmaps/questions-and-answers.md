### KUBERNETES-SECRETS-AND-CONFIGMAPS-JN-01

- [ ] **Question:** What is ConfigMap, and why does it matter in Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps?
> **Covered in:** [Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps — ConfigMap](README.md#configmap)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Non-secret key/file configuration consumed via API, env or projected volume. **Mechanism.** The mechanism receives configuration or runtime input, validates preconditions, changes or derives state, and exposes status to the next component. Synchronous parts return a direct outcome; asynchronous parts need durable status, reconciliation and idempotency because the original caller may disappear. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows the expected state transition and its owner, timestamp and reason. Invalid input, stale state, conflicting writers, hidden limits and dependencies that partially complete are the most useful failure categories to distinguish. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which ConfigMap matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### KUBERNETES-SECRETS-AND-CONFIGMAPS-JN-02

- [ ] **Question:** What is Secret, and why does it matter in Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps?
> **Covered in:** [Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps — Secret](README.md#secret)

**Answer:** **Definition.** API object whose data is base64-encoded and requires encryption/RBAC controls. **Mechanism.** At runtime a caller presents or derives an identity, the enforcement point gathers identity, resource and request context, and applicable rules produce allow or deny. The effective decision is the intersection of all guardrails; encryption protects bytes but does not replace authorization, and audit records explain which decision was made. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence includes a short-lived attributable identity, narrowly scoped access and an audit event for the intended resource. Failures commonly come from expired credentials, mismatched trust, an overriding deny, wrong resource scope or key/certificate lifecycle problems; widening access may hide the cause while creating a breach path. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Secret matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### KUBERNETES-SECRETS-AND-CONFIGMAPS-JN-04

- [ ] **Question:** What is Projected volume, and why does it matter in Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps?
> **Covered in:** [Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps — Projected volume](README.md#projected-volume)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Kubelet updates mounted data eventually using atomic directory/symlink behavior. **Mechanism.** A write normally passes validation and authorization, enters a buffer or transaction, reaches a durable medium, and may then replicate or become visible to readers. Caches and indexes accelerate access but introduce freshness and eviction behavior; snapshots preserve a point-in-time representation but application consistency depends on write ordering. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence combines capacity, latency, error, replication and integrity signals with a tested read or restore. Typical failures include exhausted bytes or inodes, throttling, stale replicas, corrupt metadata, topology mismatch, lost encryption keys and backups that exist but cannot reconstruct the application within RPO/RTO. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Projected volume matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### KUBERNETES-SECRETS-AND-CONFIGMAPS-JN-07

- [ ] **Question:** What is Encryption at rest, and why does it matter in Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps?
> **Covered in:** [Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps — Encryption at rest](README.md#encryption-at-rest)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Kubernetes encryption at rest transforms selected API resource values before the API server stores them in etcd. It protects raw etcd data or snapshots from directly revealing those values but does not prevent an authorized API read. **Mechanism.** The API server evaluates encryption providers in configuration order. The first provider writes new data and matching providers can read older ciphertext; envelope KMS providers use an external key service for key protection. Changing configuration affects new writes, so existing objects require a controlled rewrite to migrate ciphertext. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Test key availability, rotation, API read/write behavior and snapshot recovery. Losing key access can make the control plane unable to read protected objects; leaving `identity` first silently stores plaintext, and encrypting secrets does not fix overly broad RBAC or secret exposure in workloads and logs. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Encryption at rest matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

**Answer:** **Purpose.** `kubectl create configmap NAME --from-file=config.yaml --dry-run=client -o yaml` reads Kubernetes API desired/status state or recent reconciliation evidence without treating a single `Ready` value as proof of the user journey. **Mechanism.** The relevant Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps concept is External secrets/CSI: Synchronizes or mounts secret-manager values with availability/identity trade-offs. The command reads one representation of desired or observed state; it does not by itself prove that every controller, dependency or user path is healthy. **Evidence.** Confirm identity, account/project/cluster, location and exact resource first, then preserve IDs, status/reason fields, timestamps and exit status. Compare them with source-controlled intent and a known healthy baseline, and correlate the nearest event, metric, log, trace or audit record. **Failure and trade-off.** Empty output, permission denial, stale state and an unavailable dependency require different next checks; immediately mutating the target destroys evidence and may widen impact. **Production example.** During a Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps rollout, an operator uses this check to decide whether the fault is in accepted configuration, reconciliation or the live data path, and verifies the final repair from the original client.

## Mid-level — normal, comparison and configuration questions

### KUBERNETES-SECRETS-AND-CONFIGMAPS-MN-01

- [ ] **Question:** Compare ConfigMap with Secret. When would each change your design?
> **Covered in:** [Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps — ConfigMap](README.md#configmap)

**Answer:** **Definitions.** ConfigMap: Non-secret key/file configuration consumed via API, env or projected volume. Secret: API object whose data is base64-encoded and requires encryption/RBAC controls. **Mechanism.** In Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps, compare where each acts in the request or resource lifecycle, which state it owns, whether its result is synchronous or eventually reconciled, and how callers observe completion. They may be complementary layers rather than substitutes. **Decision criteria.** Select using workload shape and protocol, identity and tenant isolation, failure-domain independence, consistency and recovery contract, latency/throughput limits, team ownership and total cost. **Failure evidence.** State which metric, event, status field or controlled test would distinguish failure of one from the other, and how rollback differs. **Production example.** For a production Kubernetes Secrets and ConfigMaps design, use the simpler option until measured SLO, isolation, scale or recovery evidence crosses an explicit threshold; document that threshold so the decision can be revisited instead of becoming permanent folklore.
