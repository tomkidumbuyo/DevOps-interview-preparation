## Junior — normal conceptual and code questions

### DISTRIBUTED-SYSTEMS-JN-01

- [ ] **Question:** What is Partial failure, and why does it matter in Distributed systems?
> **Covered in:** [Distributed systems — Partial failure](README.md#partial-failure)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Means one component or message can fail while others continue, so a timeout cannot prove whether a remote side effect occurred. **Mechanism.** A caller assigns a deadline and attempt budget, classifies an error, applies bounded backoff with jitter only when retry is safe, and propagates capacity limits upstream. Circuit breakers, bulkheads and load shedding prevent one dependency from consuming every worker; recovery probes and gradual traffic return avoid a second overload. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows bounded queues, stable retry ratios and recovery within an objective. Unbounded retries, identical timeouts at every layer, non-idempotent replays, shared resource pools and failover to an unqualified target commonly turn a small fault into an outage. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Partial failure matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Distributed systems journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

- [ ] **Question:** What is Network partitions, and why does it matter in Distributed systems?
> **Covered in:** [Distributed systems — Network partitions](README.md#network-partitions)

**Answer:** **Definition.** The term Network partitions refers to a computing mechanism that changes how work, state or communication is represented and coordinated within Distributed systems. **Mechanism.** A real request crosses several independent states: name resolution returns an address, the source selects a route and source address, link or overlay forwarding reaches the next hop, stateful or stateless policy evaluates the flow, transport establishes communication, and TLS/application protocols negotiate their own contract. The return path must also work. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence progresses layer by layer: correct name/address, expected route, permitted flow, listening endpoint, successful handshake and valid application response. Timeouts, refusals, resets and protocol errors mean different layers; packet loss, MTU, asymmetric paths, connection-state exhaustion and proxy timeout mismatch are common production failures. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Network partitions matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Distributed systems journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

- [ ] **Question:** What is Retries, and why does it matter in Distributed systems?
> **Covered in:** [Distributed systems — Retries](README.md#retries)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Repeat attempts for transient faults but require bounded budgets, deadlines, idempotency and jitter to avoid duplicate effects and retry storms. **Mechanism.** The mechanism receives configuration or runtime input, validates preconditions, changes or derives state, and exposes status to the next component. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows the expected state transition and its owner, timestamp and reason. Invalid input, stale state, conflicting writers, hidden limits and dependencies that partially complete are the most useful failure categories to distinguish. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Retries matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Distributed systems journey rather than an administrative green status alone.
