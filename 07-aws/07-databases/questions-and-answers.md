### BRANCH-DATABASES-JN-01

- [ ] **Question:** What is DB instance, and why does it matter in Databases?
> **Covered in:** [Databases — DB instance](README.md#db-instance)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Managed engine compute/storage inside subnet/parameter/security configuration. **Mechanism.** The mechanism receives configuration or runtime input, validates preconditions, changes or derives state, and exposes status to the next component. Synchronous parts return a direct outcome; asynchronous parts need durable status, reconciliation and idempotency because the original caller may disappear. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows the expected state transition and its owner, timestamp and reason. Invalid input, stale state, conflicting writers, hidden limits and dependencies that partially complete are the most useful failure categories to distinguish. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which DB instance matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Databases journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### BRANCH-DATABASES-JN-03

- [ ] **Question:** What is Cluster volume, and why does it matter in Databases?
> **Covered in:** [Databases — Cluster volume](README.md#cluster-volume)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Replicated distributed storage is shared by writer/readers. **Mechanism.** A write normally passes validation and authorization, enters a buffer or transaction, reaches a durable medium, and may then replicate or become visible to readers. Caches and indexes accelerate access but introduce freshness and eviction behavior; snapshots preserve a point-in-time representation but application consistency depends on write ordering. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence combines capacity, latency, error, replication and integrity signals with a tested read or restore. Typical failures include exhausted bytes or inodes, throttling, stale replicas, corrupt metadata, topology mismatch, lost encryption keys and backups that exist but cannot reconstruct the application within RPO/RTO. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Cluster volume matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Databases journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### BRANCH-DATABASES-JN-04

- [ ] **Question:** What is Writer endpoint, and why does it matter in Databases?
> **Covered in:** [Databases — Writer endpoint](README.md#writer-endpoint)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Follows current writer and should be used for write-capable connections. **Mechanism.** A real request crosses several independent states: name resolution returns an address, the source selects a route and source address, link or overlay forwarding reaches the next hop, stateful or stateless policy evaluates the flow, transport establishes communication, and TLS/application protocols negotiate their own contract. The return path must also work. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence progresses layer by layer: correct name/address, expected route, permitted flow, listening endpoint, successful handshake and valid application response. Timeouts, refusals, resets and protocol errors mean different layers; packet loss, MTU, asymmetric paths, connection-state exhaustion and proxy timeout mismatch are common production failures. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Writer endpoint matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Databases journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### BRANCH-DATABASES-JN-05

- [ ] **Question:** What is Partition key, and why does it matter in Databases?
> **Covered in:** [Databases — Partition key](README.md#partition-key)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Hashes items across physical partitions and must distribute request volume. **Mechanism.** At runtime a caller presents or derives an identity, the enforcement point gathers identity, resource and request context, and applicable rules produce allow or deny. The effective decision is the intersection of all guardrails; encryption protects bytes but does not replace authorization, and audit records explain which decision was made. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence includes a short-lived attributable identity, narrowly scoped access and an audit event for the intended resource. Failures commonly come from expired credentials, mismatched trust, an overriding deny, wrong resource scope or key/certificate lifecycle problems; widening access may hide the cause while creating a breach path. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Partition key matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Databases journey rather than an administrative green status alone.
