# Application Load Balancer — interview questions and answers

## Junior — normal conceptual and code questions

### APPLICATION-LOAD-BALANCER-JN-01

- [ ] **Question:** What is Listener, and why does it matter in Application Load Balancer?
> **Covered in:** [Application Load Balancer — Listener](README.md#listener)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Accepts a protocol/port and default action under TLS policy/certificates. **Mechanism.** The mechanism receives configuration or runtime input, validates preconditions, changes or derives state, and exposes status to the next component. Synchronous parts return a direct outcome; asynchronous parts need durable status, reconciliation and idempotency because the original caller may disappear. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows the expected state transition and its owner, timestamp and reason. Invalid input, stale state, conflicting writers, hidden limits and dependencies that partially complete are the most useful failure categories to distinguish. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Listener matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Application Load Balancer journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### APPLICATION-LOAD-BALANCER-JN-04

- [ ] **Question:** What is Health check, and why does it matter in Application Load Balancer?
> **Covered in:** [Application Load Balancer — Health check](README.md#health-check)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Controls endpoint admission and should represent readiness without excessive dependency coupling. **Mechanism.** A caller assigns a deadline and attempt budget, classifies an error, applies bounded backoff with jitter only when retry is safe, and propagates capacity limits upstream. Circuit breakers, bulkheads and load shedding prevent one dependency from consuming every worker; recovery probes and gradual traffic return avoid a second overload. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows bounded queues, stable retry ratios and recovery within an objective. Unbounded retries, identical timeouts at every layer, non-idempotent replays, shared resource pools and failover to an unqualified target commonly turn a small fault into an outage. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Health check matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Application Load Balancer journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Authentication action matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Application Load Balancer journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### APPLICATION-LOAD-BALANCER-JN-07

- [ ] **Question:** What is gRPC/WebSocket, and why does it matter in Application Load Balancer?
> **Covered in:** [Application Load Balancer — gRPC/WebSocket](README.md#grpcwebsocket)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Long-lived/streaming protocols require timeout, draining and client retry alignment. **Mechanism.** A real request crosses several independent states: name resolution returns an address, the source selects a route and source address, link or overlay forwarding reaches the next hop, stateful or stateless policy evaluates the flow, transport establishes communication, and TLS/application protocols negotiate their own contract. The return path must also work. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence progresses layer by layer: correct name/address, expected route, permitted flow, listening endpoint, successful handshake and valid application response. Timeouts, refusals, resets and protocol errors mean different layers; packet loss, MTU, asymmetric paths, connection-state exhaustion and proxy timeout mismatch are common production failures. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which gRPC/WebSocket matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Application Load Balancer journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

The command reads one representation of desired or observed state; it does not by itself prove that every controller, dependency or user path is healthy. **Evidence.** Confirm identity, account/project/cluster, location and exact resource first, then preserve IDs, status/reason fields, timestamps and exit status. Compare them with source-controlled intent and a known healthy baseline, and correlate the nearest event, metric, log, trace or audit record. **Failure and trade-off.** Empty output, permission denial, stale state and an unavailable dependency require different next checks; immediately mutating the target destroys evidence and may widen impact. **Production example.** During a Application Load Balancer rollout, an operator uses this check to decide whether the fault is in accepted configuration, reconciliation or the live data path, and verifies the final repair from the original client.

### APPLICATION-LOAD-BALANCER-MN-01

- [ ] **Question:** Compare Listener with Listener rule. When would each change your design?
> **Covered in:** [Application Load Balancer — Listener rule](README.md#listener-rule)

**Answer:** **Definitions.** Listener: Accepts a protocol/port and default action under TLS policy/certificates. Listener rule: Ordered conditions on host/path/header/query/method/source select actions. **Mechanism.** In Application Load Balancer, compare where each acts in the request or resource lifecycle, which state it owns, whether its result is synchronous or eventually reconciled, and how callers observe completion. They may be complementary layers rather than substitutes. **Decision criteria.** Select using workload shape and protocol, identity and tenant isolation, failure-domain independence, consistency and recovery contract, latency/throughput limits, team ownership and total cost. **Failure evidence.** State which metric, event, status field or controlled test would distinguish failure of one from the other, and how rollback differs. **Production example.** For a production Application Load Balancer design, use the simpler option until measured SLO, isolation, scale or recovery evidence crosses an explicit threshold; document that threshold so the decision can be revisited instead of becoming permanent folklore.
