## Junior — normal conceptual and code questions

### AMAZON-ECR-JN-01

- [ ] **Question:** What is Repository, and why does it matter in Amazon ECR?
> **Covered in:** [Amazon ECR — Repository](README.md#repository)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Regional namespace with IAM/resource policy and encryption. **Mechanism.** The mechanism receives configuration or runtime input, validates preconditions, changes or derives state, and exposes status to the next component. Synchronous parts return a direct outcome; asynchronous parts need durable status, reconciliation and idempotency because the original caller may disappear. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows the expected state transition and its owner, timestamp and reason. Invalid input, stale state, conflicting writers, hidden limits and dependencies that partially complete are the most useful failure categories to distinguish. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Repository matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Amazon ECR journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### AMAZON-ECR-JN-04

- [ ] **Question:** What is Lifecycle policy, and why does it matter in Amazon ECR?
> **Covered in:** [Amazon ECR — Lifecycle policy](README.md#lifecycle-policy)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Expires images by ordered selection rules and can remove rollback artifacts if careless. **Mechanism.** At runtime a caller presents or derives an identity, the enforcement point gathers identity, resource and request context, and applicable rules produce allow or deny. The effective decision is the intersection of all guardrails; encryption protects bytes but does not replace authorization, and audit records explain which decision was made. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence includes a short-lived attributable identity, narrowly scoped access and an audit event for the intended resource. Failures commonly come from expired credentials, mismatched trust, an overriding deny, wrong resource scope or key/certificate lifecycle problems; widening access may hide the cause while creating a breach path. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Lifecycle policy matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Amazon ECR journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence combines capacity, latency, error, replication and integrity signals with a tested read or restore. Typical failures include exhausted bytes or inodes, throttling, stale replicas, corrupt metadata, topology mismatch, lost encryption keys and backups that exist but cannot reconstruct the application within RPO/RTO. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Cross-Region/account replication matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Amazon ECR journey rather than an administrative green status alone.
