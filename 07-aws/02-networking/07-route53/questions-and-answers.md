## Junior — normal conceptual and code questions

### AMAZON-ROUTE-53-JN-01

- [ ] **Question:** What is Hosted zone, and why does it matter in Amazon Route 53?
> **Covered in:** [Amazon Route 53 — Hosted zone](README.md#hosted-zone)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Authoritative public or VPC-associated private namespace. **Mechanism.** The mechanism receives configuration or runtime input, validates preconditions, changes or derives state, and exposes status to the next component. Synchronous parts return a direct outcome; asynchronous parts need durable status, reconciliation and idempotency because the original caller may disappear. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence shows the expected state transition and its owner, timestamp and reason. Invalid input, stale state, conflicting writers, hidden limits and dependencies that partially complete are the most useful failure categories to distinguish. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Hosted zone matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Amazon Route 53 journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### AMAZON-ROUTE-53-JN-03

- [ ] **Question:** What is Weighted routing, and why does it matter in Amazon Route 53?
> **Covered in:** [Amazon Route 53 — Weighted routing](README.md#weighted-routing)

**Answer:** **Definition.** Probabilistically distributes DNS answers and is affected by resolver/client caching. **Mechanism.** A real request crosses several independent states: name resolution returns an address, the source selects a route and source address, link or overlay forwarding reaches the next hop, stateful or stateless policy evaluates the flow, transport establishes communication, and TLS/application protocols negotiate their own contract. The return path must also work. Its guarantee applies only at its own boundary, so upstream identity, downstream dependencies and asynchronous convergence still matter. **Evidence and failure behavior.** Healthy evidence progresses layer by layer: correct name/address, expected route, permitted flow, listening endpoint, successful handshake and valid application response. Timeouts, refusals, resets and protocol errors mean different layers; packet loss, MTU, asymmetric paths, connection-state exhaustion and proxy timeout mismatch are common production failures. Compare those signals with desired configuration, workload shape and a known healthy baseline before choosing a mitigation. **Trade-off.** The choice normally balances simplicity, isolation, performance, recovery effort, portability and cost; moving a boundary solves one problem while shifting another to the caller or operator. **Production example.** A team operating a service in which Weighted routing matters records the workload and configuration baseline, injects one bounded failure in a sandbox, alerts on the first user-impact or saturation signal, and verifies recovery through the original Amazon Route 53 journey rather than an administrative green status alone.

### AMAZON-ROUTE-53-JN-08

- [ ] **Code:** **Question:** What does `aws route53 get-health-check-status --health-check-id ID` help you verify for Amazon Route 53?
> **Covered in:** [Amazon Route 53 — Worked command and configuration example](README.md#worked-command-and-configuration-example)

**Answer:** **Purpose.** `aws route53 get-health-check-status --health-check-id ID` queries the AWS service control plane; IDs, Region, status/reason fields and request attribution should be correlated with CloudTrail and service metrics. **Mechanism.** The relevant Amazon Route 53 concept is Split-horizon DNS: Public/private views can intentionally answer differently and complicate diagnosis. The command reads one representation of desired or observed state; it does not by itself prove that every controller, dependency or user path is healthy. **Evidence.** Confirm identity, account/project/cluster, location and exact resource first, then preserve IDs, status/reason fields, timestamps and exit status. Compare them with source-controlled intent and a known healthy baseline, and correlate the nearest event, metric, log, trace or audit record. **Failure and trade-off.** Empty output, permission denial, stale state and an unavailable dependency require different next checks; immediately mutating the target destroys evidence and may widen impact. **Production example.** During a Amazon Route 53 rollout, an operator uses this check to decide whether the fault is in accepted configuration, reconciliation or the live data path, and verifies the final repair from the original client.
